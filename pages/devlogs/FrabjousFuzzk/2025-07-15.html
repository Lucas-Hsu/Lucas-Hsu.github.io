Now, some of you might wonder how exactly I drew those sprites, because I told you before that I'm really a beginner at art. Well, it's simple. You just have to use the S P O N G E B O B Method:

Of course not!

  1. Pick your color palette. Since I'm drawing things with only one color, I'm just going to choose about 5 or 6 shades of the same color. The shadows should be less saturated than the parts in direct sunlight, but the highlights should also be close to white.
  2. Block out the overall shape. In my case, I choose the midtone color, and draw shapes with the largest pen size first, and then when I'm done, move down to a medium sized pen to fill in a little more detail. I then use the eraser to trim the silhouette.
  3. Fill in the shadows. I'm going to pick a darker, less saturated color, and block out the shadows. I'll also use a "feathered" pen to smoothen the transition between light and dark here, because it's a sphere.
  4. Draw the highlights. It's self explanatory. Sometimes, highlights aren't even necessary because there's enough detail already.
  5. Finally, the details. I add the door and the windows. Actually, they follow the same steps as before, just with smaller pen sizes.

Node to Joy

Other than placing locations onto the map, I also rewrote most of my code. Now, I'm focusing more on Godot's Node system, the equivalent of Unity's Components. Basically, I made each feature for objects and enemies and NPCs into a standalone node. Custom Nodes I made today include Kickable, Selectable, Teleportable, and Followable.

Kickable is for the physics objects, so that they move when you kick them, and can bump and propel other Kickable objects. By adding options such as affect_other_kickables and handle_collisions_externally, I made it support enemies like the Toes and Shells too. Now, when I need to add a new physics object, all I need to do is drag and drop the Kickable node, and set up some variables and options, without writing any code!

Selectable is very versatile too. My old cursor selection system required me to add a new Array[] for each new selectable node type, which took a lot of time, and contained lots of redundant code. Now, similarly, all I need to do is drag & drop, and choose the highlight color. I can also connect a signal so that I can handle what happens when a click is received by the Selectable, such as picking up rocks, or challenging a Fuzzk to a dance battle.

Strange Fuzzk Ritual Sighted!

Followable is what made the above ring of Fuzzks possible. Any node can easily follow a Followable by adding itself to the Followable's followers Array, and then calling get_follow_position() every frame. Here, the Fuzzks are not following the player, but rather a floating circle that follows the player. The circle has an instantaneous velocity equal to the displacement between it and the player. So basically, the Fuzzks look as if they are smoothly following the player, rather than snapping to a circular orbit around the player.

TV Minimization

Yes, I know, it's actually a personal computer.

No, the title is not inaccurate. This section is indeed about denoising (kinda). When I added the TV, somehow the audio was playing no matter how far from the player it was, and that's bad. It's noisy and annoying because you can't turn the TV off. So I had to use some simple maths to model the diminution of audio strength as distance increases.

Problem: When distance is zero, volume is at maximum, 1dB. Sounds familiar? That's the exponential function! ex=1 when e=0.

It should decrease as distance increases, so reflect it along the y-axis:

f(x)=e-x

And finally, I want the volume to go beneath zero, since in Godot -80dB is the minimum, where the video source will be completely silent, I dilate it vertically and then translate it downwards. Also, I adjust the horizontal scale to account for the in-game pixel distance units, setting the function to the following:

f(x)= 81⁢e-0.0004x -80
