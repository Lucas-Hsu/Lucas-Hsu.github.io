The animation for the Toes' death is not at all funny or silly. Rather, I think it's calm, peaceful, and maybe even a little elegant. The way the wings slowly drop to the ground like feathers, it really feels soft and comforting. After a very intense battle of kicking and stinging, thousands of flaps of the wings, the Toe finally dies. That hideous creature, finally at peace.

It's a surprise that this Toe death animation turned out so well. I just wanted my enemy carcasses to despawn like how other games do it, disappearing when you aren't looking. However, it was a bit weird to have random purple feet on the ground, so I designed the toes to be easily destroyed, so when you kick them a lot, their body breaks, and only the wings remain. The fading out me trying out how to use linear interpolation (lerp), which I eventually used on the wings dropping down, as well as the Toes' changing direction.

I think the fact that the death animation of this video game creature is so intriguing shows that the enemy is lifelike enough. Right? Without life, there can be no death. So it's pretty cool to see. A lot of the work was accomplished by the "advanced" AI system I programmed for it, but the art and animations really did the most in bringing the creature alive.

What I'm trying to say is that I was wrong, at the beginning of my planning for this game, to assume that the art doesn't matter as long as the core gameplay is fun. We don't like playing text-based games, not because we don't like reading, but beacuse a game is a sum of all it's parts. I think every single component in a game matters. That's the gameplay, the sound effects, the artwork and visual effects, the soundtrack, the story, the user interface, and maybe even the keyboard controls!

However, my time is limited, and I do not have the skills to do everything on my own. I'm nearing the end of my 7-Day development plan, and the game probably won't be in a satisfactory shape by the end of tomorrow. I still have to add the UI, the sound effects, the final bossfight, as well as the saving&loading system.

Local Spawners

The Overworld as it appears to me in the Godot Editor

One important addition to the game is the local spawner. Instead of a global spawner that keeps track of the total amount of each type of enemy in the world, and spawns them right outside view of the player, local spawners are their own isolated ecosystem. This allows for more varied ranges of enemy combinations. That is to say, it's more realistic, because the world map contains ice mountains, swamps, deserts, forests, grass plains, and a river, so it's impossible for the entire world to have the exact same random placement of enemies. Now, I can make there be more Toes along the river, and make the grass plain abundant with Shells.

User Interface

The other change I made is to the User Interface design. Instead of displaying the player's health under them, I removed it completely, and instead made it so that a vignette effect appears and gradually darkens as the player's health decreases. It's less accurate, but it's more visually appealing, and gives immediate visual feedback rather than requiring the player to read a number. If you say that healthbars don't require reading, well, they don't provide good accuracy of health value either. In fact, I completely removed health bars from enemies, and even the Fuzzks.

Stubbed by Toes

Of course, if you fail to do the correct dance move, you take damage, and the vignette effect gets darker.
